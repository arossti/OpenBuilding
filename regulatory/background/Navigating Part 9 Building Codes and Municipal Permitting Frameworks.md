### Phase 1: Established Precedent (What We Already Know)

*Context for Colleague: We have already conducted extensive primary research with Energy Advisors (EAs), sustainability consultants, and federal code officials (NRC). You do not need to research the following areas, as they will serve as the assumed baseline for our tool:*

* **The Energy Compliance Workflow:** We have already mapped how EAs handle energy modeling (e.g., using HOT2000, blower door tests) and how they interact with builders. We are operating under the assumption that EAs are the most likely candidates to run embodied carbon assessments because their existing workflow heavily overlaps with what is required.  
* **The Federal Code Development Process:** We have documented how the National Model Code is developed by the federal government and the Canadian Board for Harmonized Construction Codes (CBHCC), and how it trickles down to the Provinces and Territories (PTs).  
* **The Prescriptive vs. Performance Mandate:** We already know there is a consensus that Part 9 (residential) construction will require both a simplified "prescriptive" (checklist) pathway for small builders and a "performance" (modeled) pathway for larger or more innovative builders.

### Phase 2: New Research Directives (Where We Need You to Explore)

*Context for Colleague: We need to understand the regulatory realities outside of energy modeling. Your research should focus on how zoning, structural/safety building codes, and local municipal enforcement actually function on the ground for Part 9 buildings.*  
Here are the specific boundaries and leads to guide your research:

#### 1\. Provincial/Territorial (PT) Enforcement & Regional Variances

While the federal government writes the model code, the NRC explicitly noted that enforcement and implementation reside entirely with the Provinces and Territories (PTs), who then pass authority to local Authorities Having Jurisdiction (AHJs).

* **Research Lead:** How do PTs actually implement and enforce the National Building Code? The NRC mentioned that provinces might adopt different "tiers" of the code for different jurisdictions based on regional realities (e.g., remote or Indigenous communities that cannot easily access low-carbon supply chains).  
* **Objective:** Map out how exemptions, regional variances, and tiered adoptions are legally handled at the provincial level.

#### 2\. Zoning, Green Development Standards (GDS), and Planning vs. Permitting

Our research revealed a significant disconnect between high-level municipal planning goals and the actual building permit process. Participants noted that Green Development Standards (zoning/planning) are often managed by entirely different departments than the building officials who issue building permits.

* **Research Lead:** In places like Vancouver or Toronto, builders often negotiate "development permits" (zoning) separately from "building permits" (construction). For example, a municipality might grant density bonuses or floor-area exemptions during the zoning phase in exchange for hitting higher performance steps.  
* **Objective:** Map the timeline of a residential project through municipal offices. When does a builder interact with the planning/zoning department versus the building inspector? How are requirements like Green Development Standards legally verified before a building permit is issued?

#### 3\. Navigating Non-Energy Codes (Fire, Health, Safety) & Alternative Materials

A major barrier to lowering embodied carbon is getting novel, low-carbon materials (like bio-based materials or rammed earth) approved by local building inspectors who prioritize fire, structural, and health codes.

* **Research Lead (From NRC):** An NRC official mentioned the European model, where the code sets a high-level performance metric (e.g., "a wall needs a 60-minute fire resistance rating"), and then industry groups develop prescriptive guides to meet that metric, which the AHJ accepts.  
* **Research Lead (From Practitioners):** A participant noted that getting a prefabricated/modular home approved took a year of fighting with a local building inspector who demanded factory signatures and structural engineering reports because it didn't fit the standard Part 9 box.  
* **Objective:** Research the approval process for alternative materials or methods (like prefab) in Part 9\. How do local inspectors currently verify that a novel material meets structural or fire safety codes? What is the role of the Canadian Construction Materials Centre (CCMC) in this approval chain?

#### 4\. Triggers for Existing Buildings and Renovations

Because our tool may eventually need to account for circularity, deconstruction, and retrofits, we need to understand the regulatory triggers for existing buildings.

* **Research Lead:** Participants noted that building codes for renovations operate differently—"nothing happens until something happens." A major renovation is only triggered when a certain threshold of the building is touched (e.g., "two systems or three systems").  
* **Objective:** Map out the permitting triggers for renovations, additions, and demolitions in the Part 9 space. What legal mechanisms dictate whether a builder must bring an entire house up to current code versus just the room they are working on?
